Bathing water quality in Scotland is monitored and classified by the Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA) under the Bathing Waters (Scotland) Regulations 2008, which implement the EU Bathing Water Directive. Scotland has 89 designated bathing waters — mostly coastal beaches, with a small number of inland sites.

The classification system

Each designated bathing water receives an annual classification based on samples collected throughout the bathing season (typically May to September). There are four categories:

  • Excellent — consistently very low levels of pollution; the highest standard.
  • Good — low levels of pollution; safe for swimming under normal conditions.
  • Sufficient — meets minimum standards; safe for swimming but with more variability.
  • Poor — fails to meet minimum standards; swimming is not recommended.

Classifications are based on a four-year rolling dataset, so a single poor year does not immediately drop a site to Poor status. Sites that fail to reach Sufficient classification must display a pollution advisory notice and work towards improvement.

What is monitored

SEPA collects water samples throughout the season and tests for bacterial indicators of faecal contamination — primarily Escherichia coli and intestinal enterococci. Samples are also recorded for visible litter and sewage-related debris, and these are shown in the dashboard alongside the annual classification.

Common sources of pollution

Bathing water pollution in Scotland typically comes from:

  • Agricultural runoff carrying animal waste into rivers and coastal waters.
  • Combined sewer overflows (CSOs) that discharge during heavy rainfall.
  • Septic tanks and private sewage systems near the coast.
  • Urban surface water drainage carrying contaminants to the sea.
